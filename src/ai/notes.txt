# 1.0 Kinematic Movement Algorithms
Kinematic movement algorithms use static data (position and orientation, no velocities) and
output a desired velocity. The output is often simply an on or off and a target direction, moving
at full speed or being stationary. Kinematic algorithms do not use acceleration, although the
abrupt changes in velocity might be smoothed over several frames. This can be simplified further
by forcing the orientation of the character to be in the direction of travel.

When we discuss an algorithm getting the 'steering behaviour' for an entity, we mean the
algorithm calculating the following structure's values:

class KinematicSteeringOutput:
    velocity: Vector
    rotation: float



## 1.1.1 Seek
> The steering behaviour requested for the entity is a directed on a straight line TOWARDS the given target.
> Intended for chasing a target (see 1.1.1.2 Arrival).

A kinematic seek behavior takes as input the character’s static data and its target’s static data.
It calculates the direction from the character to the target and requests a velocity along this
line. The orientation values are typically ignored, although we can use the newOrientation
function found on page 51 to face in the direction we are moving.

### 1.1.1.1 Flee
> The steering behaviour requested for the entity is a directed on a straight line AWAY FROM the given target.



### 1.1.1.2 Arrival
> Seeking a STATIONARY target.

Seek is intended for chasing a target - it will never reach its goal. If the entity does reach is goal,
this can be a problem as the velocity requests will cause the entity to move at full speed, likely
causing it to overshoot the target and constantly wiggle/orbit it. There are two options to solve this:

1. We can just give the algorithm a large radius of satisfaction and have it be satisfied if it gets
closer to its target than that.
2. We could slow the character down as it reaches its target, making it less likely to overshoot.
These options can be blended (the second option can still experience wiggle).

Algorithm found on page 62 (Steering Implementation).



## 1.1.1 Wandering
> The steering behaviour only adjusts the entity's orientation, entity moves forward relative to their
orientation at a constant velocity.

A kinematic wander behavior always moves in the direction of the character’s current
orientation with maximum speed. The steering behavior modifies the character’s orientation,
which allows the character to meander as it moves forward

# 2.0 Steering Behaviours
Steering behaviors extend the movement algorithms in the previous section by adding velocity
and rotation.

> Steering behaviour determine how an entity changes rotation and velocity to get to a target.



# 2.1 Variable Matching
The simplest family of steering behaviors operates by variable matching: they try to match
one or more of the elements of the character’s kinematic to a single target kinematic.
Variable matching behaviors take two kinematics as input: the character kinematic and
the target kinematic.

## 2.1.3 Align
Align tries to match the orientation of the character with that of the target. It pays no attention
to the position or velocity of the character or target. This steering behavior does not
produce any linear acceleration; it only responds by turning.
Align behaves in a similar way to arrive. It tries to reach the target orientation and tries
to have zero rotation when it gets there.

## 2.2.1 Pursue and Evade
> Seek for long distance targets, predicts the target's future position and uses it as the target.
> At short distances, seek works - it is not necessary to predict the target's future position.

Instead of aiming at a target's current position, we need to predict where it will be at some time
in the future and aim toward that point.
If the character is moving slowly, or the target is a long way away, the prediction time
could be very large. The target is less likely to follow the same path forever, so we’d like to
set a limit on how far ahead we aim.

Algorithm found on page 70.

## 2.3.2 Separation
> Entities moving close to each other but NOT crossing paths.

The separation behavior is common in crowd simulations, where a number of characters are
all heading in roughly the same direction. It acts to keep the characters from getting too close
and being crowded.
It doesn’t work as well when characters are moving across each other’s paths (see 2.3.3 Collision
Avoidance).
Most of the time, the separation behavior has a zero output; it doesn’t recommend any
movement at all. If the behavior detects another character closer than some threshold, it acts
in a way similar to an evade behavior to move away from the character. Unlike the basic evade
behavior, however, the strength of the movement is related to the distance from the target.

Algorithm found on page 83.



## 2.3.3 Collision Avoidance
> Entities that are crossing paths.

The collision avoidance behavior assumes that targets are spherical. It is interested in avoiding
a trajectory too close to the center point of the target.

Collision Avoidance has a number of implementations relating to the search area in front of the
entity. These include:
> Cone search in front of the entity (page 86).
> Predictive collision checks (page 87). Algorithm found on page 88.
> Ray solutions:
    > Parallel rays coming from the entity's sides.
    > Large central ray with small whisker rays either side.
    > Whisker rays only.
    > Single central ray only.

## 2.4 Combining Steering Behaviours
A moving character usually needs more than one steering
behavior. It needs to reach its goal, avoid collisions with other characters, tend toward safety
as it moves, and avoid bumping into walls.



## 2.4.1 Blend and Arbitration
> Blending is calculating all the steering outputs and combing their results in some way.
> Arbitration is letting one or more steering behaviours decide (have complete control).
There are two methods of combining steering behaviors: blending and arbitration.
Each method takes a portfolio of steering behaviors, each with its own output, and generates
a single overall steering output.

Blending does this by executing all the steering behaviors and combining their results using
some set of weights or priorities. This is sufficient to achieve some very complex behaviors, but
problems arise when there are a lot of constraints on how a character can move.

Arbitration selects one or more steering behaviors to have complete control over the character.
There are many arbitration schemes that control which behavior gets to have its way.

Blending and arbitration are not exclusive approaches, however. They are the ends of a
continuum.

## 2.4.2.1 Flocking and Swarming
The original research into steering behaviors by Craig Reynolds modeled the movement patterns
of flocks of simulated birds (known as “boids”) [Hence the name - flocking].
The flocking algorithm relies on blending three simple steering behaviors: move away
from boids that are too close (separation), move in the same direction and at the same velocity
as the flock (alignment and velocity matching), and move toward the center of mass of the
flock (cohesion).

Weighted blending suffers from a number of problems:
> Stable Equilibria (Page 100).
> Constrained Environments (Page 101).
> Nearsightedness (102).



## 2.4.2.2 Priorities
A variation of behavior blending replaces weights with priorities. In a priority-based system,
behaviors are arranged in groups with regular blending weights. These groups are then placed
in priority order.
